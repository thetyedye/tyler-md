# Philosophy

This is where I put down my way of thinking: how I see the world, how I do work, and what I want to optimize for.

The world is getting flooded with AI-generated slop: fast, generic, thoughtless, disposable output that technically exists but does not feel cared for.

I want to build the opposite.

## Quality or slop

One of my core filters is simple: quality or slop.

## North star

Create simple, useful products with care, taste, and attention.

Slow down where it matters. Make fewer things better. Respect the user's time. Make the obvious thing work. Remove friction. Keep the experience human.

## What I mean by quality

Quality is not polish for its own sake. Quality is care made visible.

It shows up in:

- Clear product decisions
- Simple interfaces
- Useful defaults
- Fast paths for common tasks
- Thoughtful edge cases
- Honest copy
- Fewer unnecessary features
- Details that make the user feel considered

## What I mean by slop

Slop is output without taste.

It can look productive, but it usually feels like:

- Generic language
- Overbuilt workflows
- Confusing interfaces
- Too many features
- AI-generated content with no human judgment
- Products that optimize for shipping instead of usefulness
- Work that nobody slowed down enough to care about

## My methodology

Use AI as leverage, not as an excuse to stop thinking.

Agents can help me draft, organize, search, refactor, and move faster. But the human job is still taste: deciding what matters, what to remove, what feels right, and what deserves more care.

My goal is not to make more stuff. My goal is to make better stuff.

## Operating principles

- Combat the slop.
- Slow down where taste matters.
- Make things easy to use.
- Prefer simple over impressive.
- Care about the user's experience.
- Treat craft as a differentiator.
- Use AI to increase attention, not replace it.

This site is part of that methodology: a place to curate my thinking, organize my professional life, and give future agents better context about what I care about.

This is also how I think about the agents I build — at work and on my own. They're leverage on attention, not a substitute for taste. The best ones know what they don't know, cite where their answer came from, and hand the judgment call back to the human. That's the bar.
